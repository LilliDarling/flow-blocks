🧠 Energy-first, not time-first

Your brain doesn't run on a clock — it runs on energy and interest. Schedule block types (deep focus, light tasks, admin) rather than specific tasks. When you arrive at a block, pick from your menu based on what your brain can actually handle right now.

🚪 Transition buffers are airlocks

Task-switching with ADHD is like turning a cargo ship — it takes time. Those 10-15 minute buffers between blocks aren't wasted time; they're the space your brain needs to let go of one thing and gear up for the next. Protect them fiercely.

🍽️ The menu approach

Give yourself 2-3 task options per block. "Deep focus: write report OR organize files OR research project." Your brain gets autonomy and novelty, which makes engagement way more likely than a single rigid assignment.

⚓ Anchor, don't chain

Pick 2-3 non-negotiable anchors for your day (morning review, one deep block, wind-down). Let everything else flex around those. Fewer rigid commitments = less guilt when things shift, which they will. And that's fine.

✅ Track what you DID, not just what you planned

ADHD brains chronically undercount their output. The "done list" at the bottom of your day view exists for this reason. Mark things complete as you go — you'll be surprised how much you actually accomplish.

🔋 Low energy ≠ lazy

Some days your battery is at 30%. That's data, not a character flaw. On low-energy days, lean into light tasks and admin. Trying to force deep focus when you're depleted just creates shame spirals. Respect the battery level.

🔄 The "good enough" schedule

Perfectionism kills ADHD productivity systems. If you followed 60% of your plan, that's a win. The goal isn't robotic adherence — it's gentle structure that catches you when executive function dips.
